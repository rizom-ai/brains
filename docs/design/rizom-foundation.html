The Argument

Social contracts, not constitutions,
hold institutions together.

The frameworks organizing modern work — the firm, the org chart, the employment contract, the manager-as-conduit — are inheritances from the industrial era. They were brilliant solutions to problems we no longer have, and they are silently failing on problems we do.

What actually holds an institution together isn't its constitution. It's the social contract underneath — the unwritten agreements about who can ask whom for what, who decides, who's responsible when something breaks. Ours are broken. And industrial-era frameworks can't be patched into health.

We need new ones, built from different principles. That's what we're working on.

Public Infrastructure

The infrastructure
under the community.

The foundation stewards the AI coordination infrastructure that the Rizom community runs on — Rover, the personal-knowledge agent, and Recall, the shared-memory layer that connects them. Both are open-source, openly governed, and deliberately designed to be operated by communities rather than owned by platforms.

We treat them as public infrastructure, not product. That framing matters — it's what makes the foundation legible to civic funders, digital-rights organizations, and the European institutions thinking about what sovereign AI actually looks like in practice.
