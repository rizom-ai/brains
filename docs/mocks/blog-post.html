Notes on field receipts

A working studio is held together less by its outputs than by the small, dated receipts of its attention.

Published
12 May 2026
Reading

Every studio I have worked in keeps two kinds of records: the polished ones that face the client, and the tangled ones that face the work itself. The first set gets filed neatly. The second set gets lost — and with it, almost everything that mattered about how a decision actually got made.

I have come to call the lost set field receipts. Not the project brief, not the deliverable, not the retrospective. The small scraps in between: the chat thread that re-routed a week, the screenshot annotated at 1am, the voice memo on the way back from a site visit. Each one is mundane. Together they describe — with more honesty than any retrospective — how the work was actually held.

Why receipts vanish

The usual reason is friction. A receipt that requires a form to file is a receipt that does not get filed. So receipts collect in the edges of the tools that already hold them — Slack threads, terminal history, the bottom of a Notes app, scrawls on a paper map — until the tool churns and the receipt is gone with it.

The second reason is taste. Most receipts are embarrassing. They record the wrong turn, the second-guess, the question that turned out to be naïve. We are trained to file the version where we knew what we were doing — and to quietly discard the version where we were figuring it out. The figuring-out version is the one a future colleague needs.1

What a good receipt looks like

A useful field receipt has four properties. It is dated, because a receipt without a date is a rumour. It is local, meaning it sits next to the thing it describes rather than in a separate archive. It is cheap to write — under thirty seconds, ideally inside the tool you were already in. And it is honest about confidence: a receipt that hedges into unfalsifiability is no better than no receipt at all. 

2026-05-12  switched the indexer to write-through after losing
            two batches on a restart. doesn't fix the underlying
            race in commit ordering — just narrows the window.

That is four lines. It will outlive the indexer. It tells the next person why the configuration looks the way it does, and tells them what was deferred — which is the thing a future bug report will need to know.

Receipts in a brain

The reason I think about this here is that a personal knowledge system — a brain, in our terminology — is exactly the shape that wants receipts. Topics drift. Decisions reverse. The system that quietly remembers when something was true is the system that stays trustworthy over years. The system that overwrites yesterday with today's better understanding loses the most interesting information in the corpus.2

Concretely: every entity should carry a created and a history, every summary should know which version of its sources it was drawn from, and nothing should be silently replaced. That last constraint is harder than it sounds.

A small closing receipt

This essay is itself a receipt. It is dated. It is filed next to the thing it describes. It cost about an hour to write, which is the upper bound of what a receipt is allowed to cost. And it is honest about confidence: I am sure about the smallest viable practice, less sure about how to encode it in tooling, and not yet sure whether receipt will turn out to be the right word.

Either way — it is filed.
